## Portfolio Optimization & Construction Methods (183 papers)

*This collection of 183 papers is unified by a shared focus on how institutional and individual investors should construct, optimize, and manage multi-asset portfolios across varying market regimes, liquidity conditions, and liability structures. Methodologically, the papers span a wide spectrum—from classical mean-variance optimization and its extensions (higher-moment optimization, robust optimization, full-scale optimization) to machine learning-based clustering approaches (hierarchical risk parity, k-means, self-organizing maps, dynamic time warping) and simulation-based frameworks (Monte Carlo, scenario analysis, backtesting), with recurring attention to the instability of covariance estimation and its downstream effects on portfolio weights. The central research questions addressed include: how to overcome the well-documented fragility of Markowitz optimization in practice; whether alternative allocation heuristics (equal weighting, risk parity, HRP, inverse volatility) outperform optimization out-of-sample; how to incorporate illiquid and alternative assets (private equity, real estate, hedge funds) into strategic frameworks such as the total portfolio approach or endowment model; how diversification benefits evolve across regimes and globally integrated markets; and how tail risk, drawdown constraints, and liability-aware objectives should reshape portfolio construction for long-horizon investors such as endowments, foundations, and pension funds.*

## Private Markets & Alternative Investments (66 papers)

*This collection of 66 papers is unified by its focus on the performance measurement, risk characterization, and portfolio construction challenges posed by private markets and alternative investments—particularly private equity, venture capital, private debt, and real assets. Methodologically, the literature employs a range of quantitative approaches including cash-flow-based metrics (IRR, TVPI, PME/direct alpha), lagged-beta and return-unsmoothing techniques to correct for appraisal smoothing and stale pricing, factor model replication, the Takahashi-Alexander cash flow simulation framework, and illiquidity-adjusted optimization models for strategic asset allocation. The central research questions addressed include whether private equity genuinely outperforms public market equivalents on a risk-adjusted basis, how illiquidity risk and return smoothing distort reported performance and portfolio risk, how investors should size and pace private market commitments within diversified institutional portfolios, and whether GP compensation structures and fund characteristics explain cross-sectional performance variation.*

## Risk Parity & Risk-Based Allocation (15 papers)

*## Academic Summary: Risk Parity & Risk-Based Allocation

This collection is unified by a shared rejection of mean-variance optimization's reliance on expected return estimates, instead proposing allocation frameworks that equalize or explicitly budget risk contributions across assets or asset classes. The literature spans two broad methodological streams: classical risk parity approaches—including equal risk contribution, leverage-aversion arguments (Asness et al.), and maximin optimality proofs (Fisher et al.)—and hierarchical machine-learning extensions pioneered by Lopez de Prado's HRP and subsequently refined through HERC (Raffinot), hierarchical risk budgeting (Koumou), and deep reinforcement learning (Han), which exploit clustering algorithms to impose a tree-structure on portfolio construction and improve out-of-sample stability. Central research questions include whether risk parity portfolios are theoretically optimal or merely heuristically superior to capitalization-weighted and mean-variance benchmarks, how risk budgets should be defined and allocated across heterogeneous asset and risk classes, and whether hierarchical methods can further reduce estimation error, concentration, and instability that plague conventional quadratic optimizers—with empirical evidence generally supporting risk-based approaches on diversification and drawdown metrics, particularly over long horizons and in the presence of fat tails and dynamic correlations.*

## Rebalancing & Dynamic Asset Allocation (10 papers)

*These ten papers collectively examine the design, evaluation, and optimization of portfolio rebalancing strategies across multiple asset classes, united by a shared concern with how investors should maintain target allocations over time in the presence of market drift, transaction costs, and varying risk objectives. Methodologically, the literature spans mean-variance and risk-contribution frameworks (Kohler & Wittig; Zhang & Ahluwalia), threshold- and calendar-based rebalancing rules (Harjoto & Jones; Tokat & Wicas; Donohue & Yip), options-based and derivatives-assisted alternatives (Israelov & Tummala), and empirical analyses of equity return dispersion and volatility harvesting (Maeso & Martellini; Brown), with Leibowitz & Bova and Van Den End extending the inquiry to institutional policy portfolios and macroeconomic channels such as quantitative easing. ## Covariance Estimation & Correlation Dynamics (9 papers)

*## Academic Summary: Covariance Estimation & Correlation Dynamics

These nine papers collectively address the estimation, cleaning, and forecasting of covariance matrices and pairwise correlations in financial markets, united by the shared challenge that naive sample-based estimators are unreliable due to noise, limited observation windows, illiquidity, and structural non-stationarity. Methodologically, the papers span a broad toolkit including shrinkage estimation (Ledoit & Wolf), Random Matrix Theory and free probability (Bun & Bouchaud), similarity-based non-parametric filtering (Cartea et al.), network and hierarchical clustering approaches (Marti et al.; Papenbrock), and regime-conditional or factor-based modeling for time-varying correlations (Brixton et al.; Czasonis et al.), while De Jong's work specifically tackles the structural challenge of estimating covariances involving illiquid assets with sparse price observations. The central research questions revolve around how to produce well-conditioned, statistically consistent covariance estimates suitable for portfolio optimization, how to model and predict the dynamic behavior of key correlations—particularly the stock-bond relationship as it shifted from negative to positive—and how network-theoretic representations of correlation structure can yield actionable insights for risk management and asset allocation.*

## Time Series Forecasting & Deep Learning (6 papers)

*These six papers collectively address the modeling, forecasting, and synthetic generation of financial and general time series data, spanning both classical statistical frameworks and modern deep learning architectures.
